MISSION 01

ENCODE A SECRET MESSAGE

The Caesar cipher is one of the oldest encryption methods in history — Julius Caesar used it to send secret military orders. The idea is simple: shift every letter forward in the alphabet by a fixed number. Only someone who knows the shift key can decode it.

Why isn't the Caesar cipher secure today?
There are only 25 possible shifts. A computer can try all of them in less than a millisecond. Modern encryption uses keys with billions of possible values — completely different scale.

MISSION 03

INFILTRATE THE PASSWORD VAULT

Websites never store your actual password. Instead, they store its hash — a fixed-length fingerprint generated by a one-way function. When you log in, the site re-hashes what you type and compares it to the stored hash. Try it with real SHA-256 hashing, the same algorithm used by actual websites.

What about data breaches?
If a hacker steals a database, they get hashes — not passwords. To crack them, they'd need to guess millions of passwords and compare hashes. This is why long, unique passwords matter — they're much harder to guess even with the hash.
